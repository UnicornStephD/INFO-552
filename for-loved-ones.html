For Loved Ones of Al-Anon Participants

Understanding and supporting someone attending Al-Anon — without overstepping.

When someone you care about starts attending Al-Anon, it can raise questions. What are they learning? Why do they go? Is something wrong?

Al-Anon is a program designed for people affected by someone else’s drinking. It helps members focus on their own well-being rather than trying to fix or manage another person’s behavior. This might be a shift — especially if your relationship has included shared worry or problem-solving.

What You Might Notice

  • They may start setting healthier boundaries
  • They might speak less about the drinker and more about their own feelings
  • They may talk about concepts like “detachment” or “letting go”

This doesn’t mean they love anyone less. It means they’re learning to take care of themselves — something many people struggle with when dealing with someone else’s drinking.

How You Can Support Them

  • Respect their privacy — meetings are anonymous, and they may not want to share every detail
  • Let them grow in their own time — recovery is a personal process
  • Avoid giving advice unless asked — listening is often the most loving thing you can do

If you find yourself deeply affected too, you're not alone. You may even consider attending a meeting yourself to better understand what your loved one is learning — or simply to find your own support.
